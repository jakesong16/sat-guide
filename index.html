About The SAT

    General Info

  • The prestigous SAT (Scholastic Assessment Test) is a worldwide exam offered to students testing their skills across reading, grammar, and math and demonstrating how prepared they are for college.
  • Most students who take the SAT take it in their junior and senior year of high school. However, the SAT can be taken by students as low as 6th grade. Their scores will not be stored unless they are at least an eighth grader though.
  • The test consists of three sections: A reading, writing, and math (calculator + no calculator) section.
  • The exam is a multiple choice pencil and paper test with 13 written answer questions from the two math sections of the test.
  • The duration of the exam consists of a total of three hours that are divided into the three sections of the test.
  • The exam is usually offered in the months of March, April, May, June, August, October, November, and December.

    • Scoring

  • The possible scores one can score on the SAT range from 0-1600.
  • In order to score a perfect score (1600) on the SAT, one must be highly proficient in all the three subjects tested while having some top notch test taking strategies.
  • The scores are based off two subsections: Reading (Reading + Writing) and Math (Math no Calc + Math w Calc). Both subsections are individually scored out of 800 each and are then added up to get the final score.
  • The reading and writing section are both out of 400 each then get added up to get the individual reading subsection score.
  • When scoring, each SAT test can have a different scoring curve in a certain section than others based off the difficulty of that section.
  • Generally when scoring, each wrong answer is equal to 10 points taken off. However, usually in the reading section there is a curve where 8 wrong answers in that section results in a 340/400 instead of 320/400. As for the writing and math sections, there usually won't be a curve like this.
  • According to the College Board's 2021 total group report, these were the national SAT average scores: Evidence-Based Reading and Writing: 533 Math: 528 Total: 1060

    • Sign Up Guide

  • In order to sign up for an SAT test, one must create a collegeboard account and sign up through their website on the MySAT page.
  • After creating your account, enter in your personal information so you can proceed to the SAT exam sign up for future SAT test dates.
  • Each SAT exam has deadline dates to sign up for the exam. Regular registration costs 60$ while late registration (signing up after the sign up deadline) comes with some additional late fees.
  • Each exam is administered at different test centers with a certain amount of available seats depending on the test center for sign up.
  • The earlier you plan to sign up for an exam, the more likely a seat will be available for you. Seats tend to fill up quickly even months before the exam. Make sure you sign up early!

    • Taking the Test

  • When taking the exam, the order of the sections you complete goes Reading, Writing, Math no Calc, and lastly Math w Calc.
  • When completing the section you are on, you are NOT allowed to go to another section of the test.
  • The section you are currently on will not end until time is up. So if you finish early, make sure you use the extra time to double check your answers.
  • When filling out answers, you will be given an answer sheet to fill in by shading in the circle with the letter answer choice corresponding to your answer.
  • Luckily, there are two 10 minute breaks during the test. Your first one will be after the first section (reading) and your second one will be after the third section (Math no Calc).
  • When taking the test, make sure your phone or any other electronic devices are powered off or handed to the SAT administators.

The Reading Section

The Reading Section of the SAT consists of 52 questions to be answered in 65 minutes. In this section contains five passages: A literature, science, social science/study,
history, and another science section. These five sections usually (but not always) appear in that order in the SAT. Also, one of these five passages will have a part 1 and part 2 reading from
two different authors in the whole passage. The two authors will usually have two distinct perspectives on the main topic of the passage and you will have to compare them in the questions
for that passage only. As for the rest of the passages besides the part 1 and part 2 one, the questions for each of these sectons are very similar as they all follow the same guidelines of questions.
The questions in this section test you in two primary skills: Command of Evidence: your ability to find concrete evidence within the passage to support the author’s claims or answers to specific questions.
Words in Context: your ability to decipher the meanings of vocabulary words within the context of the passage, and your ability to understand how word choice influences the style and tone of a text.
As you answer the questions, you'll mostly see these specific type of questions:

  • Big Picture - ("The main purpose/idea of the whole passage")
  • Little Picture/Function - ("The author uses the words '__' in line "__" to...")
  • Inference - ("The author implies/states/regards...").
  • Vocabulary in Context - ("The word '__' in line '__' most nearly means...")
  • Author Technique - (Part 1 of the two part questions which will ask about an aspect of the text and will need evidence support to support your answer)
  • Evidence Support - (Part 2 of the two part questions which usually will ask "which choice best supports the answer to the previous question?")
  • Data Interpretation - (Science passages can have a figure/data table/graph at the end of the passage and you will have to answer a few questions according to the figure)

Reading Strategies:

  • Start with the easiest reading passages first. For example, the literature passage is the first passage of the section, but you can flip the page and start on a science passage instead if they are easier for you. This allows you to get the easier passages out the way first so you can have more time to focus on the harder passages at the end of the test.
  • Read the whole passage first then move onto the questions. For the order of the type of questions to answer: 1) Vocab 2) Line evidence Questions (Which choice provides the best evidence...) 3) Line references 4) Main idea 5) Figure questions (If the passage has them)
  • For the pt 1 and pt 2 line evidence questions, first look at the question in part 1 and then refer to the lines in answer choices in the pt 2 question and see which lines directly correlates with the pt 1 question. That should be the answer to part 2 and use that answer to go back and answer pt 1.
  • Remember, EVERY correct answer is directly stated in the passage. If an answer choice is not directly stated in the passage, it is most likely NOT the answer. This section is called Evidence Based Reading for a reason. Do NOT make assumptions or inferences that aren't backed up by evidence.
  • General rule is that if you ever run out of time or have to guess on some questions, pick the same answer (for example C) for every guess. This far increases the probability of you getting at least some questions right rather than picking different answer guesses for every question you have to guess on.

The Writing Section

The Writing Section of the SAT consists of 44 questions to be answered in 35 minutes. In this section contains four passages ranging from a wide variety of passage topics. This includes
careers, history/social studies, science, and the humanities. This section will primarily test you on four types of skills:

Command of Evidence: These types of Writing questions focus primarily on the big picture of a passage and usually ask you if want to should a sentence to the passage or not. If you should, then
you would need to provide evidence for why you are making a particular change.
Words in Context: These questions will ask if you must replace a word or phrase with a more logical choice. If not, then select "NO CHANGE"
Expression of Ideas: These questions will make you think about the various ways ideas can be expressed in words. This means
rearranging, adding, combining, or deleting sentences to improve the overall flow of a passage.
Standard English Conventions: For Standard English Conventions questions, you must correct words or phrases so they follow the basic rules of English grammar, punctuation,
spelling, and capitalization. If the highlighted word or phrase is grammatically correct, then select “NO CHANGE.”
As you answer these type of questions, you'll see mostly these specific types of questions:

  • Command of Evidence - ("At this point, the author is considering adding the following sentence. '__________' Should the author make this addition here?")
  • Words in Context - ("Which choice best maintains the tone established in the passage?")
  • Expression of Ideas - ("In context, which choice best combines the underlined sentences?").
  • Standard English Conventions - (These questions may give you an example of a phrase in the passage and give you four option choices of dfferent ways to grammatically write the phrase like if a word should have an apostrophe or not)

Writing Strategies:

  • Shortest and less redundant answer choices are most likely right, especially when the question is a Word in Context or an Expression of Ideas question.
  • If a question with significance to the main idea of the paragraph/passage has been introduced before you have read that part, read it through and then go back to answer the question. Ex: ("Which Choice best introduces the main idea of the paragraph?" For this question, read the whole paragraph FIRST and then go back and answer the question).
  • Process of elimination is CRUCIAL!
  • When double checking if you have time, make sure you look at the following sentences after each question to confirm that the answer choice makes sense. The SAT will
    sometimes utizile the following sentences after a question that can be often overlooked to trick test-takers.
  • If you ever run out of time or have to guess on some questions, pick the same answer (for example C) for every guess. This far increases the probability of you getting at least some
    questions right rather than picking different answer guesses for every question you have to guess on.

The Math No Calculator Section

The Math No Calculator Section of the SAT consists of 20 questions to be answered in 25 minutes. This section contains a wide variety of math subtopics mostly from Algebra and Geometry.
The math no calculator section will primarily test you on three types of skills:

Heart of Algebra: The Algebra content area covers the largest focus and material of the SAT Math section, accounting for approximately one-third of all Math questions.
Heart of Algebra questions focus on primarily linear equations, systems of equations, inequalities, and absolute values.
Passport to Advanced Math: There are 16 Passport to Advanced Math questions on the SAT from both no calculator and calculator sections. These advanced questions
test your understanding of the structure of equations and expressions, including your ability to rearrange and rewrite them. For these questions, you may be asked to
solve a quadratic equation, create an exponential function, or manipulate polynomials.
Additional Topics in Math: These question types are basically a catch-all for any math concept that don't fit neatly into the other
three categories. Such topics predominantly deal with geometry, trigonometry, and complex numbers.
As you answer these type of questions, you'll see mostly these specific types of questions:

  • Heart of Algebra - ("If 5x = 20, what is the value of x + 4")
  • Passport to Advanced Math - ("What is the sum of the two polynomials shown? (5x^4 + 3x + 2 -(x^3 + 5x + 1")))
  • Additional Topics in Math - ("If sinx = 3/5, what is the value of cos(90-x)?").

Math no Calc Strategies:

  • If you are having trouble on the problem, use the answer choices AGAINST the test. This means for each answer choice, assume it's right and see if it is true with the context of the problem.
  • For some questions, you can set variables equal to any number you want to as long as it fits the constraint. Ex) Which of the following equations are equal for all values of x > 0; Plug in 1 as x in the answer choices and see which choice and equation provides the same answer, meaning they are equal.
  • Process of elimination is CRUCIAL!
  • If you are ever stuck, skip the question for now and do it at the end as the final questions tend to be the hardest so don't lose precious time in the beginning.
  • If you ever run out of time or have to guess on some questions, pick the same answer (for example C) for every guess. This far increases the probability of you getting at least some
    questions right rather than picking different answer guesses for every question you have to guess on.

The Math With Calculator Section

The Math with Calculator Section of the SAT consists of 38 questions to be answered in 55 minutes. Similar to the math no calc section, Math with Calc contains most of the same variety
of subtopics with the addition of a new subtopic: Problem Solving and Data Analysis (data interpretation).
The math no calculator section will primarily test you on three types of skills:

Heart of Algebra: The Algebra content area covers the largest focus and material of the SAT Math section, accounting for approximately one-third of all Math questions.
Heart of Algebra questions focus on primarily linear equations, systems of equations, inequalities, and absolute values.
Problem Solving and Data Analysis: These type of problems cover data interpretation questions over statistics, graphs, charts, ratios, relationships, probability, and more.
Passport to Advanced Math: There are 16 Passport to Advanced Math questions on the SAT from both no calculator and calculator sections. These advanced questions
test your understanding of the structure of equations and expressions, including your ability to rearrange and rewrite them. For these questions, you may be asked to
solve a quadratic equation, create an exponential function, or manipulate polynomials.
Additional Topics in Math: These question types are basically a catch-all for any math concept that don't fit neatly into the other
three categories. Such topics predominantly deal with geometry, trigonometry, and complex numbers.
For the specific type of questions for each of the subtopics, they are the same as the math no calc section. However for the new topic (Problem Solving and Data Analysis), you'll
see these specific types of questions:

  • Problem Solving and Data Analysis - ("Based on the graph...." and then you'll be asked some interpretation questions such as identifying an equation of a line on the graph, a specific coordinate point value on the graph, etc.)
  • Problem Solving and Data Analysis - ("Based on the table...." and then you'll be asked some interprettion questions such as the probability of an event in the graph, a sample size/margin error question, the percentage of the people in one part of the graph to another part of the graph (ratio), etc.)
